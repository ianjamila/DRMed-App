● Start here

The big picture

Every patient follows the same simple path. Four teams each own one part of it, and the app passes the work from one team to the next automatically — nobody has to chase anybody.

Patient
Walks in, books, or self-registers by QR.
→
Reception
Books the visit & collects payment.
→
Lab
Runs the tests & enters results.
→
Patient
Views the result online.
→
Admin
Pays the doctor their fee.
!

The one golden rule: a result can only be released to a patient once the visit is paid. The app enforces this automatically — there's no way to release an unpaid result by accident.

● The teams

Who does what

Each role only needs to worry about their own part. Here's the short version.

Reception

Front desk
  • Register the patient & book the visit
  • Pick the tests / services
  • Collect payment, print the receipt
  • Count the cash drawer, close the day

Lab

Medtech · X-ray · Sonographer
  • Pick up the test from the queue
  • Run it and enter the result
  • Finalise so it's ready
  • Release it to the patient

Doctor

Consulting physician
  • See the patient / do the consult
  • Sign off results (pathologist), if needed
  • Their fee is tracked automatically
  • Gets paid by Admin (GCash / cash)

Admin

Owner · Manager · Bookkeeper
  • Oversee everything from the dashboard
  • Handle insurance (HMO) billing
  • Pay the doctors their fees
  • Keep the books & reports
1
PatientReception

The patient arrives

There are two ways a patient gets into the system — they can scan the clinic's QR code and register themselves, or reception types in their details. Either way it's the exact same patient record.

① Patient self-registers · QR code

Patient · before they reach the desk
  • Patient scans the clinic's QR code (on a poster, or a link you send them).
  • They fill in their own name, birthday, and contact number on their phone.
  • Their details are already waiting when they reach reception — just pull them up and confirm.

② Reception encodes

Front desk
  • Open Patients and search by name or contact number.
  • New patient? Click add a new patient and type their name, birthday, and contact.
  • Best for walk-ins who didn't scan the QR ahead of time.
★

Both lead to the same place. Whether the patient registered themselves by QR or reception typed it in, the record is identical — so reception can always add or correct details at the counter. The QR just saves time and typing at the desk.

However they registered, if they had an appointment, open Appointments and mark them arrived to begin the visit.

2
Reception

Create the visit

A "visit" is the patient's order for today. Reception picks the patient, then picks what they're getting — lab tests, an X-ray or ultrasound, and/or a doctor's consult.

  1. Go to Visits → New visit and choose the patient.
  2. Add services from either tab: Lab & Services for tests & imaging, Doctor for a consult. Pick one, the other, or both.
  3. On the Doctor tab, choose the attending doctor.
  4. Everything lands on one visit, one receipt, one total. Save when done.

3
ReceptionPatient

Collect payment & print the receipt

Reception takes payment (cash, GCash, Maya, card, bank transfer, or through the patient's HMO/insurance) and records it. The receipt is printed with two important things the patient will use later.

  1. On the visit, click record payment and choose how they paid.
  2. The visit flips to Paid.
  3. Print the receipt. It shows the patient's Patient ID and a one-time PIN.
  4. Tell the patient to keep these — they'll use them to view results online.

4
Lab

The lab does the work

Every test ordered shows up in the Queue. The medtech, X-ray tech, or sonographer picks it up, runs it, and enters the result.

  1. Open Queue. Tests are grouped: waiting, in progress, and done.
  2. Click a test to claim it and start working.
  3. Enter the results into the form (or attach the report).
  4. Click finalise when the result is complete and correct.

5
DoctorLab

Pathologist check (only some tests)

Certain results need a pathologist to review and sign before they go out. If a test is set up that way, it waits in the sign-off list until the doctor approves it.

  1. The pathologist opens Sign-off and reviews each finalised result.
  2. They approve and sign — their signature is added to the report.
  3. The result becomes ready to release.
i

Most routine tests don't need this step — they're ready to release as soon as the lab finalises them. Sign-off only applies to the tests configured to require it.

6
Lab

Release the result

Releasing is the moment the result becomes available to the patient. The app checks two things first, automatically.

Finalised?
The result is complete (and signed, if required).
+
Paid?
The visit is marked Paid.
→
Release ✓
Patient can now see it.
!

If the visit isn't paid, the Release button is blocked. The system simply won't let an unpaid result go out — this protects the clinic and is not something staff can override.

7
Patient

The patient gets the result

Once released, the patient can view and download their result themselves — no need to come back to the clinic for a copy.

  1. The patient goes to the patient portal and enters their Patient ID and PIN (from the receipt).
  2. They see their released results and can download the PDF.

8
Doctor

The doctor's fee builds up

When a doctor did a consult, their share (their professional fee) is worked out and tracked automatically — nobody has to compute it. Where it goes depends on how the patient paid.

Cash patient
Doctor's fee is ready to pay right away.
|
Insurance (HMO) patient
Held until the HMO pays the clinic, then it becomes ready to pay.
₱

Why insurance ones wait: the clinic hasn't received that money yet — the HMO pays later. Holding the doctor's fee until then means the clinic never pays out money it hasn't collected.

9
Admin

Pay the doctor

At the end of the day, Admin (or reception, depending on your setup) opens Pay doctors and settles up. The amounts are already worked out — you just send the money the way you normally do and tick it off.

  1. Open Pay doctors and stay on the Ready to pay tab.
  2. Send each doctor their total by GCash or cash — exactly as you do now.
  3. Click Pay this doctor, choose how you paid, and confirm.
  4. Done — they move to Already paid. Leave Waiting on insurance alone; those release on their own.

ReceptionAdmin

Close the day

At the end of the shift, reception counts the physical cash and closes out. The app compares what you counted to what it expects, and admin can see the full picture in reports.

  1. Open Cash drawer to see the running total: starting cash, money received, money paid out.
  2. Click Count & close day, enter the cash you actually counted, and the app shows any difference (over/short).
  3. Admin reviews the day in the dashboard and reports.

● Admin deep-dive
Admin

Insurance (HMO) billing & tracking

When a patient is covered by an HMO, the clinic isn't paid at the counter — the insurer pays later. This is how Admin bills each HMO and keeps track of the money owed until it lands.

!

An HMO visit is "paid" enough to release results — but the money isn't in yet. Choosing the HMO on the visit lets the patient get their results, but the clinic is now owed that amount by the insurer. Admin's job is to collect it.

The HMO money journey

HMO visit
Reception picks the insurer + approval / authorisation no.
→
Unbilled
Results released; the clinic now needs to invoice the HMO.
→
Billed
Admin sends the claim to the HMO.
→
HMO pays
Admin records the payment; income is realised.
→
Settled ✓
Claim closed; the doctor's fee becomes ready to pay.

What Admin does, step by step

  1. Spot the unbilled ones. Once results are released, the visit appears under All unbilled — amounts the clinic can invoice but hasn't yet.
  2. Bill the HMO. Group the released tests into a claim and submit it — recording the date and how you sent it (email, courier, the HMO's online portal, etc.).
  3. Chase the slow ones. Claims that sit past that insurer's agreed terms show up under All aging and the Aging matrix, flagged red. Follow these up.
  4. Record the payment. When the HMO pays, enter the payment and how much went to each item. The amount owed drops and the income is recognised.
  5. Handle short-pays & denials. If the HMO pays less than billed, or rejects an item, decide the leftover: bill the patient the difference, or write it off.

₱

Short-pay or denial: if an HMO covers only part — say ₱1,000 of a ₱1,500 claim — Admin splits the leftover ₱500: either add it to the patient's bill, or write it off as a loss. A claim is only "done" once every peso is accounted for.

i

The doctor's fee waits for the HMO. On an insurance visit, the doctor's fee stays under Waiting on insurance (Step 8) until the HMO settles — then it moves to Ready to pay. This stops the clinic from paying out money it hasn't collected.

★

Daily nudge: the dashboard shows an "HMO unbilled aged 90+" total — claims left un-invoiced for over 90 days. If that figure climbs, open All unbilled and get them billed.

● Just in case

Plain-word glossary

We kept jargon out of the app. Here are the few words you'll still see, in plain terms.

Visit
One patient's order for the day — the tests, imaging, or consult they're getting, plus the payment.
Patient ID
The patient's permanent code (e.g. DRM-004821). Printed on the receipt; used to log in online.
QR registration
A code patients can scan to fill in their own details before reaching the desk. Optional — reception can always type the details in instead. Same record either way.
PIN
A one-time code on the receipt that lets the patient open their results online. Shown once; reception issues a new one if lost.
Release
Making a finished result available to the patient. Only possible once the visit is paid.
HMO
The patient's health insurance. The clinic bills them and gets paid later, instead of the patient paying in full.
Professional fee (doctor's fee)
The doctor's share of a consult. Worked out and tracked by the app; paid out by Admin.
Cash drawer
The reception till. The app tracks starting cash, what came in, and what went out, so the day balances.
